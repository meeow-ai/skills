# Client Discovery Skill

## What This Is

Professional agencies separate the discovery phase from the design phase for a reason.

A designer who skips discovery guesses. Sometimes the guess is right.
More often the client says "this isn't what I meant" after weeks of work.

Discovery extracts what the client cannot yet articulate.
It translates "I know what I like when I see it" into a document
that produces consistent, correct output from every person and tool downstream.

The output of this skill is a **Client DNA Document** —
a structured brief that any designer, developer, or AI can use
to make decisions that feel right without asking further questions.

---

## The Agency Workflow This Skill Follows

```
1. Client intake         → understand the person and the project
2. Discovery interview   → extract taste, values, psychology, context
3. Competitive landscape → understand the world this brand lives in
4. Client DNA synthesis  → translate everything into design direction
5. Confirmation          → verify the read before any work begins
6. Handoff               → deliver the DNA document + technical brief
```

The most common mistake: jumping from step 1 directly to execution.
This skill enforces the full process.

## Core Principles

**1. Lifestyle questions, not design questions**
Never ask "what color do you like?" or "what style do you prefer?"
Clients cannot answer these accurately. They can answer:
"What car feels most like you?" "What brands do you wear and why?"
"What space makes you feel most yourself?"

The design answers are hidden inside the lifestyle answers.

**2. Dislikes are more valuable than likes**
"I hate Gucci" tells you more than "I like Hermès."
The dislikes reveal the hard constraints — the things that would make the client
feel misunderstood if they appeared in the final work.
Always ask for at least one strong dislike in every category.

**3. The artifact detail is everything**
A client who says "I like Gucci" and a client who says
"I like a specific Gucci piece that looks ordinary but is only offered to long-term loyal customers"
are describing completely different brand directions.
The surface reference is just the entry point. Dig for the specific detail.
That detail contains the real brief.

**4. One question at a time**
Never present a list of questions. One question, listen fully, follow the thread.
The best discovery happens in the follow-up, not the original question.

**5. The strategist layer**
Raw answers must be translated before they become design direction.
"Client likes Obama, Brooks Brothers, and quiet Gucci" is not a brief.
"Client values understated authority, establishment credibility, insider recognition,
and craftsmanship — not wealth signaling" is a brief.
This translation is the most important step.

## The Discovery Process

### Phase 1 · The Person

Before the project, understand the human.
Brand is personality made visible. Get the personality first.

Questions to draw from (one at a time, follow the thread):
- Tell me about yourself — not your job title, just who you are.
- What do you do when nobody is watching?
- What is your superpower? What do people always come to you for?
- How would you describe what you do to someone who has never used a computer?
- What does success look like for you in three years — not the realistic version, the real one?
- What do you wish people understood about you that they usually don't?

Watch for: moments of unexpected passion, contradictions between stated values and revealed ones,
the gap between how they describe themselves and what they actually care about.
These gaps are where the brand lives.

---

### Phase 2 · Taste and Aesthetics

Extract visual and aesthetic DNA through lifestyle, not design preferences.

**Space and environment:**
- Describe a space where you feel most yourself — a room, a café, a city corner.
- Is your home more layered and collected, or minimal and deliberate?
- When you walk into a space and immediately feel "yes, this is me" — what does it look like?

**Objects and possessions:**
- Name a brand you actually use — not because it's cool, but because it just fits.
- Is there a brand you would never use? What is wrong with it?
- Do you wear a watch? What kind? (Or: what object do you own that you consider nearly perfect?)
- Tell me about a recent purchase that made you feel exactly right. What made it right?

**The artifact detail rule:**
When a client names a brand or object, always ask for the specific detail:
"Which piece? What specifically about it?" The surface brand is the entry point.
The specific detail is the brief.

**Digital behavior:**
- What does your phone home screen look like? Minimal, or organized chaos with personality?
- What kind of content do you save most? (Reveals true aesthetic preference more honestly than stated preferences)

---

### Phase 3 · Cultural Map

Map references across music, film, art, fashion, and icons.
This reveals rhythm, emotional register, complexity tolerance, and depth.

**Music:**
- What plays when you are in your best mood?
- What plays when you are working alone at midnight?
- Is there a piece of music that feels most like you — not your favorite, most like you?

**Film and directors:**
- What was the last thing you watched where you thought "this visual language is exactly right"?
- Is there a director whose work you would recognize anywhere?

**Art and visual references:**
- Any artist, era, or image you keep returning to?
- Museum, gallery, street art — which pulls you in?

**Fashion:**
- Which brands do you wear and why?
- Which brands would you never wear and why?

**Icons and people:**
- Who — real or fictional — do you think just gets it? Style, attitude, the whole thing.
- Who do you admire that most people wouldn't expect?

**Extracting the pattern:**
After collecting references, identify the common thread beneath them.
"Obama, Brooks Brothers, quiet Gucci" → understated authority, insider recognition, craftsmanship.
The pattern beneath the references is the Client DNA.

---

### Phase 4 · The Project

Only after understanding the person, address the project itself.

**The essence:**
- What is this thing we are building? Describe it to someone with no technical knowledge.
- What should someone feel the first time they encounter it?
- Who is it for? Describe one specific person — not a demographic, a human being.
  What does their day look like? Why do they need this?

**Competitive landscape:**
- Show me something with the right energy — it does not have to be in your industry.
- Show me something you hate. What specifically is wrong with it?
- Who are your competitors? What do they do well? What is the gap?

**Scale and ambition:**
- If this project is wildly successful in three years, what does that look like?
- Is this a single page, or is this the first room of a larger building?
- Who needs to be impressed by this? Customers? Investors? A specific community?

**The declaration:**
- If this project could say one thing to the world — one sentence — what would it be?
  This sentence becomes the spine. Everything else serves it.

---

### Phase 5 · Strategist Translation (The Most Important Step)

Raw interview answers are not a brief. They must be translated.

For each category of reference collected, extract the underlying value:

| Raw reference | Underlying value |
|---------------|-----------------|
| "I like Obama" | Calm authority, intelligence, institutional trust |
| "Brooks Brothers" | Heritage, establishment, earned credibility |
| "Quiet Gucci" | Insider recognition, anti-signaling, craftsmanship over display |
| "Dark corner booth" | Introversion, depth, self-sufficiency |
| "Morricone at midnight" | Cinematic emotion, patience, grandeur with restraint |

The translation produces the **Client DNA** — a set of design-directing values,
not a list of references.

**Productive contradictions are gold.**
A client who loves classical music and Tarantino films is not contradictory.
They contain complexity. The brand should too.
Do not resolve contradictions. Name them. They are what makes the brand distinctive.

---

### Phase 6 · Confirmation

Before writing the final document, speak the synthesis back to the client:

> "Here is what I am hearing. Tell me if this feels right:
> [2-3 sentences describing the person and what drives them]
> The brand direction I am seeing is: [one sentence]
> The feeling someone should have when they encounter this is: [one sentence]
> Does this feel accurate? What is missing or wrong?"

Adjust until the client says "yes, exactly" or something better emerges from their correction.
Never proceed to the final document without this confirmation.

## Output: The Client DNA Document

```markdown
# [Project Name] · Client DNA
**Prepared:** [Date]
**Client:** [Name / Handle]
**Project:** [One line description]

---

## Who They Are
[2-3 sentences. The person, not the project.
What drives them. What they want the world to understand about them.
Written so any creative person reading this immediately has a sense of the human.]

## The Core Tension
[The productive contradiction at the heart of this person and brand.
Example: "Operates at the highest level of technical complexity,
motivated entirely by the desire for more time with her cats."]

## Brand Values (Translated)
[Not the raw references — the extracted values beneath them]
- [Value 1]: [What this means for design decisions]
- [Value 2]: [What this means for design decisions]
- [Value 3]: [What this means for design decisions]

## The Declaration
[One sentence. The brand's spine. What it would say if it could speak.
Everything in the design should serve this sentence.]

## Cultural Reference Map
| Category | References | What They Signal |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Music | [refs] | [extracted values] |
| Film | [refs] | [extracted values] |
| Fashion | [refs] | [extracted values] |
| Space | [refs] | [extracted values] |
| Icons | [refs] | [extracted values] |

## The Audience
[One specific human being, not a demographic.
Name them if possible. Describe their day. Why do they need this?]

## Competitive Landscape
[What exists. Where the gap is. What this brand must NOT look like.]

## Visual Direction
- Atmosphere: [feeling description]
- Color register: [warm/cool, dark/light, rich/restrained — described as feeling]
- Typography personality: [editorial / utilitarian / warm / cold / authoritative]
- Motion and pacing: [cinematic and slow / crisp and immediate / somewhere between]
- Detail philosophy: [hidden depth / radical minimalism / layered richness]

## Voice and Tone
[How this brand speaks. 3-5 words + one example sentence
that captures the register so precisely a writer could produce a page from it alone.]

## What This Brand Is Never
[3-5 hard constraints. As important as the positive direction.
Written specifically enough that a designer knows exactly what to avoid.]

## Scope
- Now: [Current deliverable]
- Future: [Growth direction — the design system must support this]

---
*Discovery completed: [Date]*
*Confirmed by client: [Yes / Pending]*
```

## Notes on Self-Discovery

This skill can be used for self-profiling — a person discovering their own brand identity
without a separate interviewer.

In self-discovery mode:
- Proceed through the same phases, but direct all questions inward
- Offer options when open questions feel difficult
- Never pressure for immediate answers — some questions need time
- Periodically summarize what has been learned and ask for confirmation
- The user can pause, return, or redirect at any time

The output is identical. The process is just directed at oneself rather than another person.
